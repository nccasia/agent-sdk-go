#### Field operations manual

This manual is deliberately written as one undifferentiated wall of text. It is large
enough to cross the layered-reading threshold, yet it exposes no navigable structure: there
are no top-level (`#`/`##`/`###`) headings a table of contents could anchor on, only flat
`####` markers and prose. A well-built reference file would let a reader jump to a named
section; this one forces a full read or nothing. The bench treats this as a parse defect —
a large file that cannot yield a usable table of contents is not navigable, and a skill that
ships one cannot funnel its content.

#### Arrival and check-in

When arriving on site, confirm your identity at the gate and collect the visitor badge that
matches the work order. The badge color encodes the zones you may enter: green for the yard,
amber for the assembly floor, red for the high-voltage enclosure. Do not enter a zone whose
color exceeds your badge. Sign the entry log with the time of arrival, your full name, the
work order number, and the name of the site contact who authorized the visit. If the site
contact is unavailable, wait at the gate; never proceed on a verbal assurance from anyone
who is not on the work order. Stow personal electronics in the provided locker before
crossing into an amber or red zone, because radio-frequency emissions can interfere with the
calibration of the bench instruments and with the telemetry the site relies on.

#### Equipment handling

Handle every instrument as though it were freshly calibrated, because it usually is.
Transport cases must stay closed until you reach the bench, and the bench must be cleared of
food, liquids, and loose paper before a case is opened. Lift instruments from the base, never
by a protruding probe or connector, and set them on the anti-static mat that lives at every
station. Before powering anything on, inspect the cable for nicks and the connector for bent
pins; a single bent pin can short a board that costs more than a month of your salary. When
you are done, power down in the reverse order you powered up, coil the cables loosely rather
than tightly, and return each instrument to the case slot cut for its shape. A case with an
instrument in the wrong slot is a case that will crush an instrument the next time it is
closed.

#### Site safety

Safety is the one area where there is no discretion. Wear the hard hat, the safety glasses,
and the steel-toed boots in every zone above green, with no exceptions for short visits.
Know where the nearest eye-wash station, fire extinguisher, and emergency stop are before you
begin work, not after something goes wrong. The emergency stop cuts power to the entire bay,
so use it for genuine emergencies and never as a convenient off switch. If you smell ozone or
hot insulation, stop, hit the emergency stop, and evacuate to the muster point in the yard;
do not investigate. Report every near-miss, however minor it felt, because the near-miss you
shrug off today is the incident report someone else writes tomorrow.

#### Working at height

Any work above two meters requires a harness clipped to a rated anchor, a spotter at ground
level, and a tool tether on everything you carry up. A dropped wrench from four meters lands
with enough force to crack a skull, which is why nothing goes up unsecured. Inspect the
harness webbing for fraying and the carabiner gate for smooth action every time, and retire
any harness that has arrested a fall even once. Ladders are for access, not for work: if a
task takes more than a moment, use a platform. The spotter's job is to watch the worker, not
the work, and the spotter does not leave the base of the structure until the worker is back
on the ground.

#### Electrical work

Treat every conductor as live until you have personally locked it out and verified zero
voltage with a meter you tested against a known source immediately before and after. Lockout
is personal: your lock, your key, and no one removes your lock but you. The verification step
is not a formality; meters fail, and the only way to trust a reading is to prove the meter
works on a known live source first. Discharge stored energy in capacitors before touching a
board, and remember that a powered-down supply can hold a lethal charge for minutes. When in
doubt, do not touch it; find the person whose name is on the lockout and ask.

#### Hazardous materials

Solvents, adhesives, and cleaning agents live in the flammables cabinet and return there the
moment you finish with them. Read the safety data sheet before opening a container you have
not used before, and never decant a chemical into an unlabeled bottle. Used rags soaked in
solvent go in the sealed metal bin, never the general waste, because a pile of solvent rags
can ignite on its own through slow oxidation. Spills are contained first with the absorbent
granules from the spill kit, then reported, then cleaned; the order matters because a spill
that spreads while you go to find someone is a much larger problem than the one you started
with.

#### Handover and departure

Before you leave, write the handover note while the work is fresh: what you did, what you
changed, what you did not finish, and what the next person needs to watch. A handover note
written from memory the next morning is a handover note full of holes. Return your badge,
sign the exit log with the departure time, and confirm that every lockout you applied has
been removed or formally transferred to the incoming shift. Leave the bench cleaner than you
found it. The last thing you do on site is the first impression the next visitor forms of the
team, and a tidy bench is a safe bench.

#### Escalation

When something exceeds your authority or your competence, escalate rather than improvise. The
escalation path runs from the on-site contact to the shift lead to the duty engineer to the
site manager, and you do not skip a rung unless the rung above is unreachable and the matter
is urgent. An honest "I am not sure and I have stopped" is always the right answer, and it is
always cheaper than the confident mistake that the rest of this manual exists to prevent.
